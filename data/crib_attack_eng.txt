          ENIGMA CRYPTOANALYSIS MODULE: GUIDE

Welcome to the educational cryptoanalysis module!
This module presents the methods that enabled the cracking 
of the "Enigma" during World War II at Bletchley Park.

You will learn the principles of the Enigma cipher 
machine, the techniques and strategies used to decrypt it, 
and the role these efforts played in the historical context.

 AVAILABLE ANALYSIS METHODS:                                
 ------------------------------------------------------------
[1] Frequency Analysis
[2] Checking the "x != E(x)" Property
[3] Finding Positions for Crib Attack

------------------------------------------------------------
 1. FREQUENCY ANALYSIS                                    
------------------------------------------------------------
This method helps determine the frequency of occurrence 
of individual letters in the encrypted text. This aids in 
analyzing the cipher and identifying its characteristics 
for further decryption.

The histogram visually demonstrates the distribution of 
letter frequencies, making it easier to compare with 
frequency characteristics of natural languages.

In ordinary text, letters are distributed unevenly. The most 
common letters in German and English are 'E', 'N', 'I', 'S', 
and 'R'. If the cipher is strong, the letter distribution 
should be close to uniform. Significant deviations may indicate 
weak machine settings or that the message is short.

Compare the resulting graph with reference frequencies of the 
German language from the 1940s. If the distribution is uniform, 
this indicates the use of a high-quality polyalphabetic cipher.

------------------------------------------------------------
 2. THE "LETTER DOES NOT ENCRYPT TO ITSELF" PROPERTY
------------------------------------------------------------
The fundamental principle of Enigma is simple: pressing the 
'A' key never lights up as 'A' on the lamp panel. This occurs 
due to the reflector and the double passage of the electrical 
signal.

To verify the device's operation, enter a pair 
"Plaintext <-> Ciphertext". If at least one letter matches 
at the same position, this is not Enigma output or an error 
was made during input/configuration.

Example of correct pair: RITA -> LBRO (no matches)
Example of error:        RITA -> RFWQ (R -> R incorrect encoding!)

------------------------------------------------------------
 3. CRIB ATTACK METHOD AND LOOP SEARCH
------------------------------------------------------------
Historical context: cryptanalysts were familiar with standard 
phrases in German military messages. These recognizable fragments 
are called "cribs".

Program algorithm:
1. The encrypted text is analyzed to find possible positions 
   for the start of the crib.
2. Positions where letters from the crib match ciphertext 
   letters at the same positions are excluded, as this 
   contradicts the x != E(x) principle.
3. The remaining positions are considered potential locations 
   for forming "loops", which were used in the electromechanical 
   "Bombe" machine to test various rotor settings.

The following data is required for input:
   * Ciphertext: complete encrypted text consisting of letters A-Z 
     with no spaces.
   * Crib: assumed fragment of the original text, example, WETTER.

 Result: a list of indices where the word could theoretically be 
   located. These positions should be used in the emulator for 
   manual adjustment of rings and initial parameters.

------------------------------------------------------------
 HISTORICAL EXAMPLES OF CRIBS
------------------------------------------------------------
* WETTER                    -> weather report (daily!)
* KEINEBESONDERENEREIGNISSE -> "nothing special" (filler phrase)
* ANXDIEGRUPPE              -> "to the group command..." (X = space)
* HEILHITLER                -> mandatory greeting at the end
* XREPORTX                  -> start/end message markers
* EINSZWEIDREI              -> numbers spelled out (connection check)
------------------------------------------------------------
 IMPORTANT INPUT RULES
------------------------------------------------------------
1. Use uppercase Latin letters (A-Z).
2. Spaces, digits, and punctuation marks are ignored or can 
   be manually removed before input.
3. For Crib Attack, the crib length must be strictly less than 
   the ciphertext length.
4. If the program responds "No positions found" - possibly the 
   crib is incorrect or the text is too short.
5. Complete enumeration of settings (5 rotors * 26^3 positions * 
   * 26^3 rings) requires billions of years on a regular PC. 
   This module demonstrates cracking principles, not automatic
   brute force.
